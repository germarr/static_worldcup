Prediction Methodology

Mathematical framework for World Cup 2026 match outcome predictions

Abstract

This document describes the mathematical methodology used to predict match outcomes for the FIFA World Cup 2026. Our approach leverages prediction market data from Kalshi, specifically each team's probability of winning the tournament, to derive head-to-head match probabilities. We employ a logistic transformation to convert relative team strengths into win/draw/loss probabilities for group stage matches, and a simplified proportional model for knockout rounds where draws are not permitted.

1 Data Source

Our predictions are derived from Kalshi prediction market data, which provides the probability that each team will win the World Cup tournament. These probabilities reflect the aggregated beliefs of market participants and are continuously updated based on trading activity.

Let \(P_{\text{WC}}(T)\) denote the probability that team \(T\) wins the World Cup, expressed as a percentage.

Missing Data Fallback: Teams without Kalshi market data are assigned a baseline probability of \(P_{\text{WC}} = 0.1\%\), representing minimal tournament win chances. These teams are marked with an asterisk (*) in the interface.

2 Group Stage Match Probability

For group stage matches between Team A (home) and Team B (away), we calculate win, draw, and loss probabilities using a logistic transformation of the teams' relative strengths.

2.1 Log Ratio (Relative Strength)

We first compute the log ratio of the teams' World Cup win probabilities:

$$R = \ln\left(\frac{P_{\text{WC}}(A)}{P_{\text{WC}}(B)}\right)$$

This ratio is positive when Team A is favored, negative when Team B is favored, and zero when teams are equally matched.

2.2 Raw Win Probability (Logistic Function)

The log ratio is transformed into a raw probability using the logistic (sigmoid) function:

$$P_{\text{raw}}(A) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-k \cdot R}}$$

Where \(k = 0.7\) is a steepness parameter that controls how quickly probability changes with the log ratio. This value was calibrated to produce reasonable probability spreads for typical team strength differences.

2.3 Competitiveness Factor

Draws are more likely in closely matched games. We define a competitiveness factor:

$$C = 1 - 2 \cdot \left| P_{\text{raw}}(A) - 0.5 \right|$$

This yields \(C = 1\) for perfectly matched teams (\(P_{\text{raw}} = 0.5\)) and \(C \to 0\) for heavily mismatched teams.

2.4 Draw Probability

The draw probability is scaled by competitiveness, with a base rate of 26% (approximate historical average for international football):

$$P(\text{Draw}) = 0.26 \cdot C$$

2.5 Final Win Probabilities

The remaining probability mass (after accounting for draws) is distributed proportionally:

$$P(A \text{ wins}) = P_{\text{raw}}(A) \cdot (1 - P(\text{Draw}))$$ $$P(B \text{ wins}) = (1 - P_{\text{raw}}(A)) \cdot (1 - P(\text{Draw}))$$

Note that these probabilities always sum to 1: \(P(A \text{ wins}) + P(\text{Draw}) + P(B \text{ wins}) = 1\)

3 Expected Points Calculation

For group standings prediction, we calculate the expected points for each team across their three group stage matches.

3.1 Points per Match

Using standard FIFA point allocation (3 for win, 1 for draw, 0 for loss), the expected points for Team A in a single match is:

$$E[\text{Points}_A] = 3 \cdot P(A \text{ wins}) + 1 \cdot P(\text{Draw}) + 0 \cdot P(A \text{ loses})$$

Simplified:

$$E[\text{Points}_A] = 3 \cdot P(A \text{ wins}) + P(\text{Draw})$$

3.2 Total Expected Points

A team's total expected points is the sum across all three group matches:

$$E[\text{Total Points}] = \sum_{i=1}^{3} E[\text{Points}]_{\text{match } i}$$

Teams are ranked within each group by expected points. Ties are broken alphabetically by team name.

4 Knockout Stage Probability

In knockout rounds, matches cannot end in a draw (extra time and penalties determine a winner). We use a simplified proportional model that redistributes what would be draw probability.

4.1 Win Probability (No Draws)

For a knockout match between Team A and Team B:

$$P(A \text{ advances}) = \frac{P_{\text{WC}}(A)}{P_{\text{WC}}(A) + P_{\text{WC}}(B)}$$ $$P(B \text{ advances}) = \frac{P_{\text{WC}}(B)}{P_{\text{WC}}(A) + P_{\text{WC}}(B)}$$

This approach directly uses the World Cup win probabilities as a proxy for overall team strength, normalizing them to sum to 100% for each matchup.

4.2 Bracket Progression

The predicted winner of each match advances to the next round. This creates a deterministic bracket where the team with the higher probability always advances. The bracket progresses through:

  • Round of 32 (32 teams → 16 winners)
  • Round of 16 (16 teams → 8 winners)
  • Quarterfinals (8 teams → 4 winners)
  • Semifinals (4 teams → 2 winners + 2 for 3rd place)
  • Third Place Match (2 semifinal losers)
  • Final (2 semifinal winners)

5 Third Place Qualification

In the 2026 World Cup format with 48 teams in 12 groups, the top 8 third-place teams advance to the knockout stage.

5.1 Ranking Criteria

All 12 third-place teams are ranked by:

  1. Expected Points (descending)
  2. Alphabetical by team name (tiebreaker)

The top 8 teams in this ranking advance to the Round of 32.

6 Edge Cases

Both teams have zero probability

If both teams lack market data, group stage matches return \(P(A) = P(B) = 33\%\), \(P(\text{Draw}) = 34\%\). Knockout matches return \(P(A) = P(B) = 50\%\).

One team has zero probability

The team with data is heavily favored: \(P(\text{favored wins}) = 65\%\), \(P(\text{Draw}) = 20\%\), \(P(\text{underdog wins}) = 15\%\).

Equal probabilities

When \(P_{\text{WC}}(A) = P_{\text{WC}}(B)\), the log ratio \(R = 0\), yielding \(P_{\text{raw}} = 0.5\), maximum competitiveness, and the highest draw probability.

7 Limitations

  • No home advantage: The model does not account for home field advantage, which may be significant for host nations (USA, Mexico, Canada).
  • Static probabilities: Predictions use a snapshot of market data and do not update dynamically as the tournament progresses.
  • No head-to-head history: Historical matchup data between teams is not considered.
  • Simplified knockout model: The knockout probability model does not account for the different dynamics of extra time and penalty shootouts.
  • Market efficiency assumption: We assume prediction market prices accurately reflect true probabilities, which may not always hold.
